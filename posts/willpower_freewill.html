Does Willpower Relate to Free Will?

BA Philosophy Thesis - University of Groningen (2024) | Grade: 9/10

An interdisciplinary investigation integrating contemporary neurological insights with philosophical theories to explore the relationship between willpower and free will.

Table of Contents:

1. Introduction
Introduces the central research question and David Goggins as a case study for extraordinary willpower. Establishes the framework for examining willpower through both neuroscience and philosophy.

2. State of the Art Science and Philosophy
2.1 The Dopamine Desire Circuits: Explains the dopamine desire circuit (dDC) and control circuit (dCC), their roles in motivation and self-control.
2.2 The Anterior Mid-Cingulate Cortex: Discusses the aMMC's role in tenacity and how sustained effort enlarges grey matter in this region.
2.3 Harry Frankfurt's Theory: Examines first-order and second-order desires, and second-order volitions as foundations for understanding free will.

3. Desires
3.1 Parallels: Draws connections between Frankfurt's philosophical framework and scientific understanding of dopamine circuits.
3.2 Refuting Frankfurt's Theory: Critiques the hierarchy of desires using the concept of "resolve" and evidence from the aMMC.
3.3 Identifying with Desires: Defends Frankfurt's distinction between internal and external desires using dopamine research.

4. Free Will
Argues that willpower is essential for exercising free will, proposing that individuals with greater willpower are more free. Challenges Frankfurt's requirement for alignment between first-order desires and second-order volitions.

5. Limitations and Outlook
Discusses the finite nature of willpower as a biological constraint on free will and considers implications for quantifying freedom. Explores whether complete freedom is attainable given our biological limitations.

6. Conclusion
Synthesizes findings to show that willpower, understood through the dCC and aMMC, is essential for free will. Concludes that free will involves the ability to act on desires reflecting our true selves, free from impulsive external forces.
